## The History of Property Rights

The development of civilization and the protection of property rights go hand in hand. In societies where property rights are guaranteed, people think long-term, invest, and innovate. In societies where property rights are insecure, short-term consumption and concealment of wealth become rational choices.

Yet history shows that states have always violated property rights. Requisitions in wartime, taxes, asset confiscation, indirect expropriation through inflation — the forms differ, but the core is the same: those in power take individuals' property.

## The Limits of Traditional Property Rights

Real estate: The state can expropriate it.
Bank accounts: They can be frozen or seized.
Gold: In 1933, U.S. Executive Order 6102 forcibly confiscated citizens' gold holdings.
Stocks and bonds: Capital controls can prevent transfer abroad.

All traditional assets can ultimately be threatened by a state with physical coercive power.

## Bitcoin's Property Rights Revolution

Bitcoin changes this equation.

**Non-confiscatable**: If a private key exists in someone's mind, there is no physical way to take it. Taking Bitcoin from someone who has memorized 12 seed phrase words is like stealing a thought.

**No borders**: Bitcoin moves without geographic limitation. Capital controls that prevent assets from fleeing abroad do not work on Bitcoin.

**Transparent rules**: Bitcoin's rules are written in code and verifiable by anyone. Governments cannot arbitrarily change them.

## Real-World Cases

After the Taliban's takeover of Afghanistan, many people lost access to their bank accounts. But those who held Bitcoin could access their assets from abroad.

In the Russia-Ukraine war, many Ukrainians preserved their assets by using Bitcoin to carry wealth out of the conflict zone.

Bitcoin is not simply digital currency. It is the first technical implementation in human history of property rights that are completely independent of state power.
