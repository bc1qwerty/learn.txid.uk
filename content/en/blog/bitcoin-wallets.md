## A Wallet Doesn't Hold Bitcoin

The most common misconception about Bitcoin wallets is that Bitcoin is stored inside the wallet. In reality, Bitcoin always lives on the blockchain. A wallet doesn't hold Bitcoin — it stores **the key to access it**.

More precisely, a wallet is software or hardware that manages your private key. You need the private key to send or receive Bitcoin.

## Types of Wallets

**Hot Wallets**: Wallets connected to the internet.
- Mobile wallets: Smartphone apps (e.g., Phoenix, Muun, Blue Wallet)
- Desktop wallets: PC software (e.g., Sparrow Wallet, Electrum)
- Web wallets: Accessed via browser (including exchange wallets)

Convenient but connected to the internet, so they carry hacking risk. Suitable for small amounts or funds used frequently.

**Cold Wallets**: Wallets completely isolated from the internet.
- Hardware wallets: Dedicated devices (e.g., Coldcard, Ledger, Trezor)
- Paper wallets: Private key printed on paper (not recommended for security reasons)

Recommended for long-term storage of larger amounts.

## Custodial vs Non-Custodial

**Custodial wallets**: An exchange or service holds the private key on your behalf. Convenient, but subject to the "not your keys, not your coins" problem.

**Non-custodial wallets**: The user holds the private key directly. You have genuine ownership, but security is also your responsibility.

## Recommendations for Beginners

If you are just starting with Bitcoin:

1. Start with a mobile wallet using a small amount (Blue Wallet or Phoenix)
2. Once you have more to store, buy a hardware wallet (Coldcard or Trezor)
3. Develop the habit of physically storing your seed phrase in a safe place

More important than choosing a wallet is keeping your seed phrase safe. Lose the seed phrase, lose the wallet.
