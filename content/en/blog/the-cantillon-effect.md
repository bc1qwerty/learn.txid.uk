## Richard Cantillon's Insight

The 18th-century Irish-French economist Richard Cantillon made an important observation: when new money is injected into an economy, an inequality arises between those who **receive it first** and those who receive it later.

This is the **Cantillon Effect**.

## How It Works

Imagine a central bank prints new money and releases it into the market. Where does this money go first? To banks, large financial institutions, and companies with government contracts.

These entities receive the new money and can purchase assets before inflation spreads through the broader economy — buying stocks, real estate, and commodities before their prices rise.

This money gradually spreads through the whole economy. But by the time an ordinary worker or saver receives a raise or earns more income, prices for everything have already risen. They can buy less with the same amount of money.

## The Modern Cantillon Effect

Post-2008 financial crisis quantitative easing (QE) policies by central banks vividly demonstrated this effect.

Trillions in new money were printed and injected into the financial system. Financial institutions used this money to buy assets. Stock and real estate prices soared. Those who owned assets became wealthier, while the real wages of those without assets stagnated.

This is one of the main reasons wealth inequality has deepened globally over the past 10–20 years.

## The Solution Bitcoin Offers

Bitcoin blocks the Cantillon Effect at its source.

New Bitcoin issuance occurs predictably according to the halving schedule. Who receives it first? Miners. But mining is open competition. Anyone with mining equipment can participate.

There is no structure where a privileged group monopolistically receives new currency, as with a central bank. Bitcoin encodes fairness in the issuance process itself.
