## What Is Fiat Money

Fiat money is currency that has become money by government decree. "Fiat" is Latin for "let it be done." It holds value not because it is backed by a real asset like gold or silver, but solely on the authority of government and the trust of its citizens.

After the United States abandoned the gold standard in 1971, all major world currencies became fiat money. What were the consequences of this transition?

## The Danger of Unlimited Issuance

In a fiat money system, governments and central banks can issue currency without limit. There is no constraint from gold reserves.

History shows this power has been abused. Money has been printed in massive quantities to fund wars, to paper over financial crises, and to implement populist policies.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, the United States issued approximately 40% of all the dollars ever created within just two years. The result was the inflation everyone experienced.

## Inflation: The Invisible Tax

When the money supply increases, the purchasing power of each unit falls. This is inflation. Inflation quietly and continuously erodes people's savings.

The problem is that it is invisible. When the government directly levies a tax, it is visible and provokes resistance. But diluting purchasing power by printing money happens without most people noticing.

## A Debt-Based Monetary System

Modern fiat money exists through debt. New money is created when banks issue loans. This system can only be sustained if the economy continuously grows and debt keeps expanding.

This is structurally unsustainable. When debt levels reach their limits, the system faces crisis. The 2008 financial crisis is a prime example.

## Bitcoin as an Exit

Bitcoin is one answer to all these problems. Its supply is mathematically limited; unlimited issuance is impossible. No government, no bank can print Bitcoin.

Understanding the flaws of fiat money is the first step to understanding Bitcoin.
