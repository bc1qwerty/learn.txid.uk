## What Is Mining

Bitcoin mining does two things simultaneously: it issues new Bitcoin, and it verifies transactions and records them on the blockchain.

The name "mining" comes from gold mining. Just as gold miners dig into the earth to obtain gold, Bitcoin miners expend computing power to obtain new Bitcoin.

## The Technical Process of Mining

Miners perform a computational task to find a **hash** satisfying specific conditions. The work is simple repeated trial — feeding in random numbers as fast as possible and checking whether they meet the conditions.

The miner who first finds a qualifying hash earns the right to add a new block to the blockchain, and receives newly issued Bitcoin as a reward. This is the **block reward**.

Currently (after the 2024 halving), the block reward is 3.125 BTC, and one block is created approximately every 10 minutes.

## Mining Difficulty Adjustment

The Bitcoin network automatically adjusts mining difficulty approximately every two weeks. When more miners join the network, difficulty increases; when miners leave, difficulty decreases.

Thanks to this mechanism, no matter how much computing power grows, Bitcoin's block generation time remains approximately 10 minutes. Bitcoin's issuance rate is mathematically controlled.

## Mining Pools

The probability of an individual finding a block alone is lower than winning the lottery. So most miners join **mining pools** — groups that combine their hash power to find blocks collectively, then split rewards according to each miner's contribution.

## Mining and Bitcoin's Security

Bitcoin network security is maintained because miners compete to calculate hashes. To forge past transactions or attack the network, one would need to control more than half the total network hash power — which in practice is nearly impossible.

Mining is not simply creating new coins. It is the core mechanism protecting Bitcoin as global payment infrastructure.
