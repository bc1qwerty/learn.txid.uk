## What Is the Halving

Bitcoin's network has an event roughly every four years where mining rewards are cut in half. This is called the **Halving**.

When Bitcoin first launched in 2009, miners received 50 BTC as a reward for each block mined. Every 210,000 blocks, this reward is halved. The first halving in 2012 brought it to 25 BTC; in 2016, 12.5 BTC; in 2020, 6.25 BTC; and in the fourth halving in 2024, it became 3.125 BTC.

This process continues until the total supply reaches 21 million, estimated to occur around 2140.

## Why the Halving Matters

The halving is the core mechanism encoding Bitcoin's monetary policy in code.

**Reduced supply**: The number of newly issued bitcoins decreases. Miners do the same work but receive fewer BTC.

**Increased scarcity**: As the rate of supply growth declines, the scarcity of already-circulating Bitcoin increases. It is like the mining rate of a gold mine being cut in half.

**Predictability**: The halving schedule is written into the code and can be known by anyone in advance. No central bank, no politician can change it.

## The Halving and Price

Historically, Bitcoin's price has tended to rise significantly after halvings. Within a year of the 2012 halving, prices increased dozens of times over; similar patterns appeared after the 2016 and 2020 halvings.

There is no guarantee this will always repeat. But the economic principle that a declining rate of supply growth can affect price, when demand is constant or rising, is straightforward.

## The Stock-to-Flow Model

Frequently discussed in relation to the halving is the **Stock-to-Flow** model. This is the ratio of the existing stock of an asset divided by the amount newly produced annually (flow).

Gold's stock-to-flow ratio is approximately 60. As halvings accumulate, Bitcoin's ratio surpasses gold's. This is the mathematical basis for Bitcoin being the most scarce monetary asset in history.
